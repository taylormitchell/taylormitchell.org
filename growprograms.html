Growing Programs

yyyy-mm-dd

This post was inspired by a tweet from Omar Rizwan:

i want to grow a program, like it's a plant that lives down in my browser console, and i take care of it and add to it over days and months and years

I love this metaphor and want to explore what it might look and feel like to play it out. What follows is a story about Alice, an avid user of a fictional notes app, and her experience growing programs within it.

Fertilizer

Alice's work involves a lot of writing, so much of her day is spent inside her favourite notes app.

As she goes about her work, she organically develops workflows which fit the way she thinks and works.

For example, she keeps an "Inbox" section at the start of each essay where she puts next steps, new ideas, etc and an "Archive" section at the bottom, where she keeps sentences and paragraphs that she needs to cut but can't bring herself to delete.

By having a consistent structure for her essays (Inbox and Archive sections) and methods for handling certain types of text (e.g. todos and cut sentences), Alice is creating fertile soil for programs to grow within.

Seeds

As time goes on, some of her workflows become core parts of her work. Especially the archiving workflow, which she uses many times a day. Although she likes having an archive of her cut sentences, she doesn't like how implementing it takes her out of the flow of writing.

She would love it if she could just select some text, hit a hotkey, and have it moved down to the archive automatically. Luckily, the notes app supports end-user scripting, so she decides to try creating one for this workflow.

Alice has some experience programming, but never in this environment, so she decides to use the apps macro recorder to record her performing the archive workflow and create a script which mimics it. The result isn't perfect, but it only takes a few modifications in the apps built-in IDE to get it working.

With that, the seed of a program is planted! She assigns it to a hot key and gets back to your work.

Sowing

Over the coming weeks, Alice creates a bunch of short scripts. None of them are very intricate, but they save her time throughout the day and allow her to focus on the writing itself.

Most of the scripts she never ends up using, while other's take root. This stage feels like sowing the seeds. You can't count on any one seed to sprout so you need to plant lots.

Tending

The scripts require a bit of tending to as she use them - fixing a bug here, adding some logic there - but this is easy to do within the app. The script lives right there in the app with you, so it's always right within reach to make changes to. There's no need to compile or deploy, the script is running live in the app so changes are reflected instantly.

When there's an error, the app provides a notification. With one, you're dropped into a debug mode which allows you to step through an find the problem.

Blooming

At some point, Alice notices that some of the scripts feel like variations of the same idea. All of them take a piece of text, and then quickly moving it to another section or document. The notes app supports creating packages, so she decides to try refactoring these scripts into one.

She creates abstractions which span the specific use cases which her scripts implement, and encapsulates them in modules. She also defines the main interface for the package: some built in defaults like "move to archive", and "move to inbox", as well as an option to search and select any section and document to move to. She surfaces the interface through the right-click menu.

Now, as she uses the package, it feels just right. It took some time and tending to for her scripts to grow, but they've now bloomed into the program that they always wanted to be.

Repotting

Eventually Alice's scripts and packages begin to settle. Her writing environment fits the way she thinks and works more than it ever has, but she's still not satisfied. It feels like there's more room to improve, but her programs seem to have hit a plateau.

There comes a time in every plants life when a change in it's living accommodations is required, and itâ€™s time for a repotting. This could be because it's outgrown its previous container or because it simply needs some new soil.

Alice knew that her programs could be so much more if allowed to grow, but they were hitting the limits imposed by their home inside the notes app.

So she decided it's time to repot them. She exports her programs from the notes app and places them into a new folder. She fires up her code editor and gets to work building a new stand-alone notes app which can grow unconstrained to fit her writing needs perfectly.
