Growing Programs

2022-03-25

This post was inspired by a tweet from Omar Rizwan:

i want to grow a program, like it's a plant that lives down in my browser console, and i take care of it and add to it over days and months and years

I love this metaphor and want to explore what it might look and feel like to play it out. What follows is a story about Alice, an avid user of a fictional notes app, and her experience growing programs within it.

Manual workflows are the seeds of programs

Alice's work involves a lot of writing, so much of her day is spent inside her favourite notes app.

As she goes about her work, she organically develops workflows which fit the way she thinks and works. For example:

  1. She keeps an "Inbox" section at the start of each essay where she keeps a list of next steps, unincorporated ideas, etc.
  2. She keeps an "Archive" section at the bottom of each essay, where she keeps sentences and paragraphs that she needed to cut but wants to keep around to maybe bring back in.

These workflows are the seeds of programs. They're well-defined and repeatable, something something that could be implemented by a computer.

But workflows like these may drastically change as you find ways to improve them or as the needs of your work changes. Since they're quick and easy to implement, Alice doesn't mind implementing them manually and find doing so provides lots of opportunities to notice how they can be improved.

Scripts are the initial sprouting of a program

As time goes on, some of her workflows become core parts of her work. Especially the archiving workflow, which she uses many times a day. Although she likes having an archive of her cut sentences, she doesn't like how implementing it takes her out of the flow of writing. Every time she wants to archive some text, she needs to select it, cut it, scroll all the way to the bottom of the document, paste it under the archive heading, and then scroll back up.

She would love it if she could just select some text, hit a hotkey, and have it moved down to the archive automatically. Luckily, the notes app supports end-user scripting, so she decides to try creating one for this workflow.

Alice has some experience programming, but never in this environment, so she decides to use the apps macro recorder to get started. She clicks "start recording macro", manually performs the steps in her archive workflow, and then clicks "stop recording macro". This creates a new script which performs exactly the actions she just did manually. The resulting script isn't perfect, but it only takes a few modifications in the apps built-in IDE to get it working how she wants.

With that, her first program has sprouted! She assigns it to a hot key and gets back to your work.

The script requires a bit of tending to as she uses it - fixing a bug here, adding some logic there - but this is easy to do within the app. When there's an error, the app provides a notification, and with one click she's dropped into a debug mode to fix it. As she tends to it, there's no need to recompile or redeploy. The program is always running live right there in the app and ready to go.

Some scripts bloom into packages

Over the coming weeks, Alice implements more and more of her workflows as short scripts. None of them are very intricate, but they save her time throughout the day and allow her to focus on the writing itself.

At some point she notices that some of the scripts feel like variations on the same idea. All of them take a piece of text, and then quickly moving it to another section or document. The notes app supports creating packages as well, so she decides to try refactoring these scripts into one.

She creates abstractions which span the specific use cases which her scripts implement, and encapsulates them in modules. She also defines the main interface for the package: some built in defaults like "move to archive", and "move to inbox", as well as an option to search and select any section and document to move to. She surfaces the interface through the right-click menu.

Now, as she uses the package, it feels just right. It took some time and tending to for her scripts to grow, but they've now bloomed into the program that they always wanted to be.

Programs can outgrow their home and must be repotted

Eventually Alice's scripts and packages begin to settle. Her writing environment fits the way she thinks and works more than it ever has, but she's still not satisfied. It feels like there's more room to improve, but her programs seem to have hit a plateau.

There comes a time in every plants life when a change in it's living accommodations is required, and itâ€™s time for a repotting. This could be because it's outgrown its previous container or because it simply needs some new soil.

Alice knew that her programs could be so much more if allowed to grow, but they were hitting the limits imposed by their home inside the notes app.

So she decided it's time to build them a new home. She exports her programs from the notes app and places them into a new folder. She fires up her code editor and gets to work building a stand-alone app which can grow unconstrained to fit her writing needs perfectly.
